Aero Component Design, CFD & Fabrication

Hytech Racing · Georgia Tech  ·  Aerodynamics  ·  2021–2023

Full design-to-hardware cycle on a Formula Student competition racecar — generating geometry, validating through CFD simulation, iterating on results, and producing physical 3D-printed components that flew on the car.

Situation

As part of Georgia Tech's Formula Student racing team, I worked on the aerodynamics group. Formula Student cars are built from scratch each year by students — every component has to be designed, validated, and manufactured in-house within a tight timeline and budget. There's no vendor sending you validated parts; you design it, simulate it, and make it yourself.

Task

Design aerodynamic components that generate measurable downforce improvement, validate them through CFD simulation, and produce physical parts ready to mount on a competition racecar.

What I Did
  1. 01
    Generated candidate geometries. Developed initial aero component designs based on target downforce and drag coefficients using CAD. Started with known airfoil families and adapted them to the car's specific constraints — ground clearance, mounting points, packaging envelope.
  2. 02
    Ran CFD analysis. Simulated airflow over each geometry. Analyzed pressure gradients, velocity distributions, and stall behavior to evaluate performance. The bottom surface accelerates flow faster than the top — lower pressure below generates downforce.
  3. 03
    Iterated based on simulation. Used CFD results to refine geometry — adjusting camber, chord length, and angle of attack until simulation outputs matched downforce targets without excessive drag penalty. Multiple design-simulate-refine cycles.
  4. 04
    Produced physical parts. Converted final validated designs into 3D-printed components. Fit-checked on the car, validated mounting geometry against the CAD model, and confirmed behavior matched simulation predictions during test runs.
